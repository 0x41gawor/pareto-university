# Lexicon Draft — L. Game Theory and Decision-Making
(Formal logic of strategic interaction)

This section introduces core concepts from game theory used to analyze
strategic interaction, conflict, cooperation, and asymmetry in politics,
economics, and war.

---

## Game Theory  
**PL:** teoria gier

**Definition:**  
A framework for analyzing situations in which outcomes depend on the
strategic choices of multiple actors.

**Why it matters:**  
Game theory formalizes strategic thinking beyond intuition or narrative.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_theory

---

## Zero-Sum Game  
**PL:** gra o sumie zerowej

**Definition:**  
A game where one actor’s gain is exactly another’s loss.

**Why it matters:**  
Common in military conflict and some competitive settings.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-sum_game

---

## Non-Zero-Sum Game  
**PL:** gra o sumie niezerowej

**Definition:**  
A game where outcomes can benefit or harm multiple players simultaneously.

**Why it matters:**  
Most economic and political interactions are non-zero-sum.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-zero-sum_game

---

## Nash Equilibrium  
**PL:** równowaga Nasha

**Definition:**  
A state in which no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing strategy.

**Why it matters:**  
Describes stable but not necessarily optimal outcomes.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nash_equilibrium

---

## Strategy Dominance  
**PL:** dominacja strategii

**Definition:**  
A strategy that yields better outcomes regardless of opponents’ choices.

**Why it matters:**  
Dominated strategies should never be played by rational actors.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominant_strategy

---

## Mixed Strategy  
**PL:** strategia mieszana

**Definition:**  
A strategy involving probabilistic choice among actions.

**Why it matters:**  
Prevents predictability in competitive settings.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_strategy

---

## Prisoner’s Dilemma  
**PL:** dylemat więźnia

**Definition:**  
A game where rational self-interest leads to suboptimal collective outcomes.

**Why it matters:**  
Models cooperation failure under anarchy.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prisoner%27s_dilemma

---

## Chicken Game  
**PL:** gra w tchórza (chicken game)

**Definition:**  
A game where players risk mutual loss unless one backs down.

**Why it matters:**  
Models brinkmanship and escalation dynamics.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicken_(game)

---

## Stag Hunt  
**PL:** polowanie na jelenia

**Definition:**  
A coordination game where mutual cooperation yields the highest payoff,
but defection is safer individually.

**Why it matters:**  
Explains trust and coordination problems.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stag_hunt

---

## Credible Commitment  
**PL:** wiarygodne zobowiązanie

**Definition:**  
A promise or threat that an actor cannot easily renege on.

**Why it matters:**  
Without credible commitment, agreements collapse.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Credible_commitment

---

## Signaling  
**PL:** sygnalizowanie

**Definition:**  
Actions taken to convey information about intentions or capabilities.

**Why it matters:**  
Reduces uncertainty in strategic interaction.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signalling_(economics)

---

## Bluff  
**PL:** blef

**Definition:**  
A signal that exaggerates strength or resolve without full backing.

**Why it matters:**  
Works only if opponents cannot verify signals cheaply.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bluff_(poker)

---

## Costly Signaling  
**PL:** kosztowny sygnał

**Definition:**  
A signal that is expensive to fake, making it credible.

**Why it matters:**  
Explains military parades, sunk costs, and public commitments.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signalling_theory

---

## Principal–Agent Problem  
**PL:** problem pryncypała i agenta

**Definition:**  
A situation where an agent’s incentives diverge from those of the principal.

**Why it matters:**  
Central to bureaucracy, corruption, and governance failure.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal%E2%80%93agent_problem

---

## Coordination Mechanisms  
**PL:** mechanizmy koordynacji

**Definition:**  
Institutions or norms enabling actors to align expectations and actions.

**Why it matters:**  
Without coordination, optimal outcomes remain unreachable.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordination_game

---

## Enforcement  
**PL:** egzekwowanie / mechanizmy karania

**Definition:**  
Systems ensuring compliance through punishment or incentives.

**Why it matters:**  
Enforcement stabilizes cooperation and institutions.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enforcement
