# Lexicon Draft — B. Anthropology and Everyday Life
(How societies work from the inside)

This section focuses on social structures, practices, and patterns of everyday life.
These concepts explain how human groups organize themselves below the level of formal institutions.

---

## Kinship  
**PL:** pokrewieństwo

**Definition:**  
A system of social relations based on perceived biological or symbolic family ties.

**Why it matters:**  
Kinship is the earliest and most fundamental coordination mechanism in human societies.

**Examples:**  
- family units  
- extended kin groups  
- lineage-based obligations  

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinship

---

## Clan  
**PL:** klan

**Definition:**  
A kinship group claiming descent from a common ancestor (real or mythical).

**Why it matters:**  
Clans scale cooperation beyond the nuclear family and provide identity, protection, and resource sharing.

**Examples:**  
- Scottish clans  
- lineage groups in tribal societies  

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clan

---

## Lineage  
**PL:** linia rodowa

**Definition:**  
A kinship system tracing descent through a single line.

**Variants:**  
- **Patrilineal** — descent through the male line  
- **Matrilineal** — descent through the female line  

**Why it matters:**  
Lineage determines inheritance, authority, and social identity.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lineage_(anthropology)

---

## Patrilineality  
**PL:** patrylinearność

**Definition:**  
Tracing descent and inheritance through the male line.

**Examples:**  
- inheritance of land or titles through fathers  

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrilineality

---

## Matrilineality  
**PL:** matrylinearność

**Definition:**  
Tracing descent and inheritance through the female line.

**Examples:**  
- inheritance through the mother’s kin  

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrilineality

---

## Tribe  
**PL:** plemię

**Definition:**  
A social group larger than a clan, often sharing language, culture, and territory.

**Why it matters:**  
Tribes coordinate multiple kin groups without formal state institutions.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribe

---

## Tribal Segmentation  
**PL:** segmentacja plemienna

**Definition:**  
A social structure where a tribe is composed of nested segments (clans, lineages) that can unite or divide depending on context.

**Why it matters:**  
Allows flexible alliances and conflict resolution without centralized authority.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Segmentary_lineage_system

---

## Chiefdom  
**PL:** społeczeństwo wodzowskie

**Definition:**  
A hierarchical society led by a chief who holds authority over multiple communities.

**Why it matters:**  
Chiefdoms represent a transitional form between tribes and states.

**Examples:**  
- Polynesian societies  
- pre-state societies in many regions  

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiefdom

---

## Sedentism  
**PL:** osiadłość

**Definition:**  
The practice of living permanently in one place.

**Why it matters:**  
Sedentism enables agriculture, surplus production, and population growth.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedentism

---

## Urbanization  
**PL:** urbanizacja

**Definition:**  
The process by which populations concentrate in cities.

**Why it matters:**  
Cities enable specialization, administration, and complex coordination.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbanization

---

## Division of Labor  
**PL:** specjalizacja pracy

**Definition:**  
The differentiation of tasks and occupations within a society.

**Why it matters:**  
Increases productivity and interdependence.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_of_labour

---

## Gift Exchange  
**PL:** wymiana darów

**Definition:**  
The circulation of goods and services without explicit market pricing.

**Why it matters:**  
Creates social bonds and obligations rather than profit.

**Examples:**  
- ceremonial gift exchanges  
- reciprocal hospitality  

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gift_economy

---

## Ethnogenesis  
**PL:** etnogeneza

**Definition:**  
The process by which a new ethnic group forms.

**Why it matters:**  
Ethnic identities are dynamic, not fixed.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnogenesis

---

## Acculturation  
**PL:** akulturacja

**Definition:**  
Cultural change resulting from continuous contact between groups.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acculturation

---

## Assimilation  
**PL:** asymilacja

**Definition:**  
The process by which a group adopts the culture of another, often dominant, group.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assimilation_(sociology)

---

## Cultural Syncretism  
**PL:** synkretyzm kulturowy

**Definition:**  
The blending of cultural or religious traditions into a new form.

**Examples:**  
- syncretic religions  
- hybrid cultural practices  

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syncretism

---

## Social Norms  
**PL:** normy społeczne

**Definition:**  
Shared expectations about acceptable behavior.

**Why it matters:**  
Norms regulate behavior without formal enforcement.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_norm

---

## Custom  
**PL:** obyczaj

**Definition:**  
Traditional patterns of behavior repeated over time.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Custom_(law)

---

## Taboo  
**PL:** tabu

**Definition:**  
A strong social prohibition against certain behaviors or topics.

**Why it matters:**  
Taboos protect core social structures and values.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taboo

---

## Ritual  
**PL:** rytuał

**Definition:**  
A formalized, symbolic action performed in a prescribed manner.

**Why it matters:**  
Reinforces group identity and shared meaning.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ritual

---

## Initiation  
**PL:** inicjacja

**Definition:**  
A rite marking the transition between social statuses.

**Examples:**  
- coming-of-age rituals  
- entry into elite groups  

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rite_of_passage

---

**Definition:**  
The hierarchical ordering of individuals or groups within a society.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_stratification

---

## Social Class

**Definition:**  
A group sharing similar economic or social position.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_class

---

## Social Status  
**PL:** status społeczny

**Definition:**  
An individual’s relative position within a social hierarchy.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_status

---

## Elite  
**PL:** elita

**Definition:**  
A small group holding disproportionate power, wealth, or prestige.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elite

---

## Patriarchy  
**PL:** patriarchat

**Definition:**  
A social system where men hold primary power.

**Note:**  
Used here in an anthropological sense, not a moral or political one.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriarchy

---

## Matriarchy  
**PL:** matriarchat

**Definition:**  
A social system where women hold primary power.

**Note:**  
Rare in strict form; often confused with matrilineality.

**Wikipedia:**  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matriarchy
